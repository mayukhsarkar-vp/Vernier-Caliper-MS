Reading Formula
Reading = Main Scale Reading + (Coinciding Vernier Division × Least Count)
  • 1
    Read the Main Scale: Note the reading just to the left of the vernier zero mark. Look for the last clearly visible graduation on the main scale.
  • 2
    Find Coinciding Line: Scan the vernier scale to find which graduation line exactly coincides (lines up) with any line on the main scale.
  • 3
    Apply Formula: Multiply the coinciding vernier division by the least count and add to the main scale reading.
  • 4
    Final Reading: The sum is your measured value with full precision. Verify units and record.
⚡ Pro Tip: Always observe the caliper scale straight-on (perpendicular to your line of sight) to avoid parallax error — the most common mistake when reading a vernier caliper.

What is a Vernier Caliper?

A precision measuring instrument that uses a vernier scale to provide measurements more accurate than a standard ruler. Invented by Pierre Vernier in 1631, it can measure to 0.02mm precision.

📏

Main Scale

The primary fixed scale graduated in millimetres (or 1/16 inches). The main scale reading is the last graduation to the left of the vernier zero mark.

🔍

Vernier Scale

The sliding secondary scale with 50 (or 25/10) divisions. The vernier principle exploits the difference between main scale and vernier divisions to measure fractions accurately.

⚖️

Least Count

The smallest measurement a caliper can make. Calculated as: LC = 1 MSD − 1 VSD = (1 mm) / (number of vernier divisions). For 50-division verniers, LC = 0.02 mm.

🟦

Outside Jaw

The lower larger jaws measure external dimensions — diameter of rods, thickness of plates, outer width of objects.

🟥

Inside Jaw & Depth Rod

The upper smaller jaws measure internal diameters (bores, holes). The depth rod at the back end measures the depth of holes and slots.

Least Count Formula
LC = 1 MSD − 1 VSD  |  LC = (Smallest main scale div) ÷ (Number of vernier divisions)
